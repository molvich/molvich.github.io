Learning Efficiently

Failure is a universal experience. Being able to fail is being able to learn, and there is nothing that makes you feel quite as helpless as programming. Due to this, countless people find themselves asking the same questions over and over again, wondering when they'll finally feel ready enough to actually get down to the programming part of programming.

Learning how to program, unfortunately, requires you to program. This monolith of a task feels impossible at first, and will feel just as hard later. Very few people are innately talented programmers, but there are many skills that most people possess that can be used to program. The main skill for any programmer is what most people refer to as 'learning to think like a programmer', or Computational-thinking [7]. Learning to think like a programmer in its simplest terms means learning how to think like a computer. This mindset change takes time, and will be something any programmer will internalize with enough practice. As a novice programmer, you have to take time to develop projects that you don't understand how to do. This process is frustrating, as most times you'll be staring at a computer screen with no idea of what your next step will be.

An important aspect of programming, and perhaps most things involving learning, is the effectiveness of group learning. Being able to collaborate with others demonstrates positive effects in relation to testing and longterm retention of concepts[2]. The language being learned may even be an aspect of learning[4], with certain concepts being more easily understandable depending on syntax and focus on these concepts that may be language dependent.

Gamification

Personally, I have found gamified semantics and other gamified programming aspects to be the most effective methods of learning. There are many websites (ie. codewars) that use game-like interfaces and activities to stimulate learning and keep attention on the subject. There are also more straightforward games, such as Reduct[3], built to work as a bridge between gaming concepts and programming problems. Gamification as a way to learn is something that many companies and people are using as an effective and proven method of teaching. Gamified semantics help break down the conceptual barriers, allowing players to directly interact and discover coding concepts through trial and error, an effective method for coding in the first place. Gamified semantics for learning programming have noted effects such as increased recall, recognition, and transference of programming concepts.[3] Coding lends itself to 'gamifying' very well, and many people in the field are already interested in games so it's not hard to find something that works for you, no matter what level you find yourself at.
